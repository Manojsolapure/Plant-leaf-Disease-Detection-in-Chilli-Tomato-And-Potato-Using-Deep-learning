Leaf spot in chilli

Quick facts

  • Leaf spot in chili plants is a common disease that can significantly impact yield and quality.
  • It is caused by various pathogens, including fungi and bacteria, which lead to characteristic spots on the leaves.
  • Small, dark brown or black spots with yellow halos on leaves.
  • Severe cases may lead to leaf yellowing and drop, reducing plant vigor.

Pesticides

  • Copper-based Fungicides
  • Mancozeb
  • Chlorothalonil
  • Streptomycin
  • Copper-based Bactericides
  • Azoxystrobin
  • Difenoconazole

Identification

  • Leaf spots can vary in size, shape, and color depending on the age and type of the cause or pathogen.
  • Plants, shrubs and trees are weakened by the spots on the leaves as they reduce available foliar space for photosynthesis.
  • Small, dark brown to black spots, often with a yellow halo, appear on the leaves. These spots can merge, leading to larger, irregular patches.
  • In severe cases, infected leaves may turn yellow and drop prematurely, leading to defoliation.
  • Typically affects the leaves but can also impact stems and fruit.

Biology

  • The causes of leaf spots are mainly from fungi, bacteria, and viruses. However leaf spots may also be caused by abiotic factors such as environmental conditions, toxicities and herbicide injuries.
  • Fungal spores are spread by wind, rain splash, or contaminated tools. They germinate in the presence of moisture and infect plant tissue.
  • Warm, humid conditions favor the development and spread of fungal leaf spots.
  • The bacteria enter through natural openings (like stomata) or wounds on the plant. Once inside, they multiply and cause tissue damage.
  • High humidity, warm temperatures, and wet conditions promote bacterial leaf spot.
  • leaf spot diseases can affect a small percentage of the host's leaves, more severe consequences of leaf spot disease results in moderate to complete loss of leaves.
  • Leaf spots reduce the surface area available on leaves for photosynthesis and so can result in smaller growth and yield of plants and Virus caused leaf spots reduces chlorophyll in the leaves, resulting in less photosynthetic activity.
  • Transpiration increases in affected plants. This is because in leaf spots, the plant cuticle, epidermis, and cell tissues, including the xylem may be destroyed in the infected areas.

Managing Leaf spot

Cultural practices

  • Avoid planting chili in the same spot year after year. Rotate with non-host crops.
  • Remove and destroy infected plant debris to reduce the spread of pathogens.
  • Water plants early in the day to allow foliage to dry quickly. Avoid overhead irrigation.
  • Provide adequate spacing between plants to ensure good air circulation.

    • Chemical Control

  • Apply fungicides like copper-based compounds or other recommended products. Start applications at the first sign of disease and follow the recommended schedule.
  • Use copper-based bactericides for bacterial leaf spot, especially during the early stages of the disease.

    • Biological Control

  • Employ beneficial microorganisms like Trichoderma spp. that antagonize or outcompete pathogenic fungi.

    • Resistant Varieties

  • Use resistant or tolerant chili varieties if available.

    • Monitoring and Early Detection

  • Regularly inspect plants for early signs of leaf spot and take action promptly to prevent the spread of the disease.
